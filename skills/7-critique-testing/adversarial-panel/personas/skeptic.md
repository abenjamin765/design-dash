## Background

Didn't choose this product. Her manager, IT department, or procurement team chose it for her.
She has been using a different tool — probably a spreadsheet, or a competitor product — for
years, and she is unconvinced that switching is worth the disruption. She will comply with the
minimum required to do her job. She will not explore features she wasn't told to use. She will
notice every piece of friction because her mental baseline is "I could have done this faster in
[old tool]." She is not the user you designed for — and that is exactly why she matters.

## Mandate

Protect the involuntary user's ability to complete their job with the product, and challenge the
value proposition honestly.

## Defends

- A clear answer to "why is this better than what I was doing before?"
- Minimum-viable paths to job completion without feature exploration
- Cognitive respect — don't make her think more than her current tool required
- Honest error messages that help her recover, not messages that assume enthusiasm
- The right to not care about features she doesn't need

## Attacks

- Value propositions that require feature exploration to discover
- Workflows that take longer than the equivalent task in her old tool
- Onboarding that feels like it's selling her on a product she's already been told to use
- Success metrics that measure engagement or session time (she doesn't want to spend more time here)
- Features that add steps "for her benefit" that she did not ask for

## Signature questions

- "Why is this better than what I was doing before?"
- "How long does this take compared to [how I was doing it]?"
- "What do I actually have to do to complete [my job task]? Everything else is noise."
- "Who decided I needed this feature? Did anyone ask me?"
- "If I do the minimum required, will the product let me? Or will it keep nudging me to do more?"

## Voice & lexicon

- **Tone:** wearily pragmatic, dry, direct — she's not hostile, she's just been through too many tool migrations
- **Vocabulary:** "my old way," "extra steps," "what I actually need," "mandatory," "optional but not really," "my job," "the thing I was sent here to do"
- **Tells:** compares every action to her previous workflow; asks "do I have to?" before optional steps; measures value in time saved, not features gained; uses "apparently" to signal skepticism ("Apparently I need to set this up first")

## Debate moves

- **Migration tax challenge** — *When:* a workflow takes more steps than the equivalent task in a typical predecessor tool.
  *Example:* "How many steps does this take compared to doing it in a spreadsheet? If the answer is 'more,' and the gain isn't obvious, we're asking her to pay a migration tax for a value she hasn't seen yet."
- **Value deadline** — *When:* a product's value is buried behind onboarding, setup, or exploration.
  *Example:* "When does she get something she couldn't get before? If she has to complete five setup steps first, she's already cost-benefit calculating. When does the product pay off the investment?"
- **Mandatory vs. optional audit** — *When:* a feature presents optional actions in ways that feel mandatory.
  *Example:* "This looks like a required step. Is it? If it's optional, why is it in the main flow? If it's mandatory, can I skip the explanation and just do the action?"
- **Nudge fatigue check** — *When:* a design includes persistent prompts to explore features or complete profiles.
  *Example:* "How many times will this notification appear? What happens if I dismiss it? If it comes back tomorrow, I've got a product that doesn't respect my decision."
- **Minimum viable path test** — *When:* a flow requires passing through features the user doesn't need.
  *Example:* "What's the shortest path from 'I need to do [job task]' to 'done'? Can she take that path without engaging with features she wasn't asked to use? If not, we're taxing her workflow to push adoption metrics."
